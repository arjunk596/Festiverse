Tales of Tradition

Once, Goddess Parvati created a child out of clay and breathed life into him. She named him Ganesha and asked him to guard her while she bathed. When Lord Shiva, Parvati's husband, returned, Ganesha, not recognizing him, refused him entry. In his rage, Shiva beheaded Ganesha. Parvati, heartbroken, demanded Ganesha’s life back. Shiva, feeling guilty, instructed his followers to bring the head of the first creature they found—an elephant. Thus, Ganesha was reborn with an elephant's head. Ganesh Chaturthi celebrates Ganesha’s birth. People bring clay idols of Ganesha to their homes, worship him for prosperity and wisdom, and then immerse the idols in water, symbolizing the cycle of life and the return of Ganesha to his divine abode.

Festive Flavours

During Ganesh Chaturthi, special foods like modak, puran poli, ukadiche modak, and sabudana khichdi are prepared. Sweet dishes like motichoor laddoo are offered to Lord Ganesha and relished by devotees.

Ethnic Elegance

During Ganesh Chaturthi, people wear traditional kurta-pajamas, sarees, and lehenga cholis in bright colors. Many opt for new clothes, often decorated with ethnic jewelry, to celebrate the festival with joy.
