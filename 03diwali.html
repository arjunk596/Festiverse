Tales of Tradition

In ancient India, there was a great king named Rama. He was exiled to the forest for 14 years, and during this time, his wife, Sita, was abducted by the demon king Ravana. With the help of his loyal companions, Rama fought and defeated Ravana, rescuing Sita. After their return to their kingdom, the people celebrated with lights, to mark the victory of good over evil. Diwali, or the Festival of Lights, was born from this joyful occasion. People light oil lamps, burst fireworks, and decorate their homes to symbolize the triumph of light over darkness. It is a time for prayers, family gatherings, and sharing sweets, as families come together to celebrate this victory of good.

Festive Flavours

During Diwali, traditional foods include sweet treats like laddoos, barfis, and gulab jamun, alongside savory snacks like samosas, kachoris, and chaklis, symbolizing joy, abundance, and togetherness.

Ethnic Elegance

During Diwali, ethnic beauty shines through vibrant traditional attire, intricate jewelry, and radiant henna designs, symbolizing cultural heritage and adding elegance to the festive celebrations.
